Runners on first and second, one out. The batter hits a ground ball to the shortstop, who steps on second and throws to first. The batter-runner beats the throw. How many outs, and what is the status of the runner forced at second?
A
One out is recorded; the runner from second is safe at third.
Two outs; the runner forced at second is out on the fielder's touch of the base.
C
Two outs; the batter-runner is also out because the force carries to first.
D
No outs; the defense must tag the runner to retire him.
CORRECT CALL
The out at second is a force, so no tag is needed — the runner is retired the instant the fielder touches the base. Because the batter-runner beat the throw to first, that force is not completed, so only the runner at second is out.

Rule myths

Rule myths

You'll hear every one of these from a bench eventually. Know the real rule before they do.
MYTH
“The hands are part of the bat.”
THE CALL
Dead ball — the batter is awarded first.
The hands are part of the batter. A pitch that strikes the hands while swinging is a hit-by-pitch unless the batter swung, in which case it is a strike.
MYTH
“The batter-runner must turn right after overrunning first.”
THE CALL
Direction alone does not put him in jeopardy.
He may turn either way returning to first. He is only liable to be tagged out if he makes an attempt toward second.
MYTH
“A tie goes to the runner.”
THE CALL
There is no tie — you judge safe or out.
The rulebook contains no "tie" provision. Call what you see: did the throw beat the runner to the bag, or not.
